A monthly, loan-level measure of how subprime auto borrowers are actually paying — built straight from the securitization tapes, not survey data — and published on-chain so it can be traded.

What's in it
Loan-level SEC ABS-EE filings from subprime auto securitizations: deals whose balance-weighted issuance FICO is below 640 and whose original pool topped $200M. Constituents are drawn from staggered 2017–2024+ vintages across the major subprime shelves (Santander Drive, Exeter, AmeriCredit, Bridgecrest, Carvana), so calendar coverage stays continuous as deals season and roll off — the live pool runs up to ~12 deals at a time, with history back to December 2016.
Handling seasoning
Subprime losses follow a hump as a deal ages, which would otherwise dominate the signal. We address it two ways. First, the index pools loans across many deals of staggered vintages and balance-weights them, so the blended pool age stays roughly flat and the level reflects credit, not the loss curve of any one deal. Second, a deal exits the pool once its balance falls below 10% of issuance, dropping the surviving-bad-loan tail of a nearly paid-down deal.
How it's calculated
Each month we recompute five balance-weighted credit metrics over the pooled loan universe — 30+ and 60+ day delinquency, Current→30+ roll rate, annualized net loss, and recovery rate. The headline stress index converts each to a rolling-24-month z-score (oriented so higher = worse) and blends them into one composite that sits near 0 in normal periods and spikes when credit deteriorates. Rebuilt monthly as new ABS-EE filings post; the COVID accommodation window (Apr–Dec 2020) is flagged but kept in the series.
On-chain margining
The index is published on-chain through an oracle, and a margined contract lets you take a cash-settled, total-return position on it: PnL = ±notional × (price − entry) ÷ entry, so a long gains when the index rises (credit worsens) and a short mirrors it. Positions are collateralized in a stablecoin (testnet MockUSDC today, designed to flip to USDC). Opening requires 20% initial margin (up to 5× leverage); if account equity falls below the 10% maintenance margin, anyone can liquidate the position for a 0.5% reward. This is currently a testnet proof-of-concept — unaudited, not for real funds.
Validation
Built only from public SEC ABS-EE filings and cross-checked against NY Fed subprime and auto-credit series (below) and rating-agency loss benchmarks. It's a quantitative research signal, not investment advice.
Worked example — one monthly mark

Illustrative figures, following 30+ day delinquency through the three steps.

  1. Pool the metric across the live deals. Sum each deal's 30+ DPD balance and its active balance, then divide the totals — large deals carry proportionally more weight.
    DealActive balance30+ DPD balance
    Santander 2023-A$300M$54M
    Exeter 2022-3$500M$95M
    AmeriCredit 2024-1$200M$46M
    Pool$1,000M$195M
    Pooled 30+ DPD = $195M ÷ $1,000M = 19.5%. (The same pooling runs for 60+ DPD, Current→30+ roll, annualized net loss, and recovery rate.)
  2. Convert to a stress z-score. Compare that 19.5% to its own trailing-24-month mean and spread. If 30+ DPD has averaged 17.0% with a 2.0% standard deviation: z = (19.5 − 17.0) ÷ 2.0 = +1.25σ (clamped at ±3). Higher delinquency is worse, so the sign stays positive; recovery is flipped, so a drop in recoveries also pushes stress up.
  3. Blend into the headline mark. Weight the five component z-scores by inverse volatility (so a noisy metric can't dominate) and sum. With z-scores of, say, +1.25 / +0.90 / +0.40 / +0.70 / +0.60, the composite lands near +0.8σ — modestly elevated that month. A reading near 0 is normal; sustained positive means subprime credit is deteriorating.
